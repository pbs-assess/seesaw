\section*{25 word summary}

Biennial survey designs can induce seesaw effects in expanded-area abundance indices when models confound spatial and temporal variation. Autoregressive temporal or spatiotemporal structures mitigate this.

\section*{Abstract}

Scientific surveys often use rotating or biennial sampling designs to efficiently allocate limited sampling effort each year but cover a larger survey domain.
Furthermore, there is growing interest in combining surveys to assess whole-stock status or climate-driven range shifts, which can also induce rotating survey coverage unless all surveys are conducted every year.
In these cases, analysts often seek an index of abundance that represents the full survey or a fish stock's domain across multiple surveys.
Model-based index standardization over an expanded spatial domain offers a potential solution.
However, we show here that applying commonly used index standardization models with independent year effects can produce unrealistic interannual oscillations, which we term ``seesaw'' effects.
Here, we diagnose the mechanisms underlying seesaw effects, evaluate conditions that modify their strength, and determine what spatiotemporal model configurations can alleviate the problem.
Fundamentally, the issue arises when models confuse spatial variation in density for temporal variation---strong density gradients in space, short spatial correlation range, and spatial gaps between survey coverage all accentuate the issue.
We demonstrate with simulated data and across four case studies that appropriately penalizing temporal or spatiotemporal variability via autoregressive correlation structures can reduce or eliminate seesaw effects.
We provide practical guidance for diagnosing these artifacts in real data and for assessing whether model-based solutions are properly tuned.
Our approach enables constructing expanded-area abundance indices from biennial and rotating survey designs that are suitable for population monitoring applications.
However, we emphasize that such rotating survey designs are suboptimal if the goal is characterization of expanded-area temporal trends.
