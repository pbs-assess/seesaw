\section*{Abstract}

% Scientific surveys often use rotating or biennial sampling designs to cover a larger spatial domain under limited annual effort.
% Surveys conducted by neighbouring jurisdictions on non-annual cycles can induce similar rotating coverage.
% In these cases, analysts often seek an index of abundance that represents the full survey or a fish stock's domain across multiple survey programs to assess whole-stock status or climate-driven range shifts.

Analysts increasingly seek to derive indices of abundance that span multiple survey domains to evaluate whole-stock status or climate-driven range shifts.
In these situations, survey data are not always sampled across the full expanded domain each year either due to rotating sampling designs or biennial sampling by neighbouring jurisdictions. 
Geostatistical index standardization offers a natural solution by predicting density in unsampled locations and years.
However, applying commonly used index standardization models with independent year effects to rotating or biennial survey data can produce spurious interannual oscillations, which we term ``seesaw'' effects.
Here, we diagnose the mechanisms underlying seesaw effects, evaluate conditions that modify their strength, and determine which spatiotemporal model configurations alleviate their effects.
We find the issue arises when models mistake spatial variation in density for temporal variation.
Strong density gradients in space, high spatiotemporal variance, short spatial correlation range, and spatial gaps between survey coverage accentuate the issue.
We demonstrate with simulated data and across four case studies that penalizing temporal or spatiotemporal variability via autoregressive correlation structures can reduce or eliminate seesaw effects.
The challenge remains choosing an appropriate level of penalization because excess penalization can attenuate interannual variability.
Our approach enables analysts to make better use of existing biennial and rotating-design survey data for population-monitoring applications.
However, even though seesaw effects can be reduced, we emphasize that rotating survey designs remain suboptimal if a goal is characterizing temporal trends across an expanded spatial domain.

\section*{Introduction}

Analysts increasingly seek to model survey data across multiple surveys in integrated ``expanded-domain'' models \citep[][]{grussImpactsDifferent2025}.
Such an approach enables assessing relative abundance across whole fish stocks where stocks cross survey borders \citep[e.g.,][]{davidsonMysteryDisappearing2026}.
It also enables calculating species distribution shift metrics using data from a whole species range, which is important when estimating quantities such as range edges REFs \citep{davidsonMysteryDisappearing2026}.
However, the data underlying such integrated models are not necessarily sampled across the full expanded spatial domain every year.
Within an agency, this can happen as a result of rotating survey designs due to logistical constraints (e.g., REF).
Across agencies, this can occur if one or more neighbouring agencies conduct a survey on a non-annual basis, for example biennially (e.g., REF). 

Geostatistical spatiotemporal models offer a natural solution to reconciling rotating or biennially sampled survey data.
By creating an expectation of fish density in space and time, such models can infer density across the expanded domain, thereby theoretically accounting for sampling that does not cover the full domain every year.
An area-weighted index of abundance can then be calculated by integrating fish density over the expanded domain \citep{thorson2015Geostatistical, thorsonSpatioTemporalModels2024,andersonsdmTMB2025}.
A classic formulation of such a model has independent year effects and independent spatiotemporal random effects \citep{thorson2015Geostatistical}.
This formulation maximizes interannual flexibility and reduces correlation in annual index estimates that may be entered into a stock assessment model.

While the properties of such independent-year spatiotemporal models applied to typical survey data are well studied \citep[e.g.,][]{thorson2015Geostatistical, yalcinExploringLimits2023}, applying these models to rotating or biennial sampling designs can result in unexpected interannual oscillations in indices of abundance, which we term here ``seesaw'' effects.
For example, in western Canada, the phenomenon has been observed for stock assessments of Yelloweye Rockfish \citep{haggarty2021yelloweyeinside}, Quillback Rockfish \citep{huynh2025quillbackoutside}, Pacific Spiny Dogfish \citep{andersonPacificSpiny2026} and Lingcod (REF). 
The effect has been observed for XX in a XX trawl survey in Norway (Figure~1).
In a transboundary context, the effect has been observed when combining survey data along the entire western U.S.-Canada coast \citep{wardWinLose2024}.
These seesaw effects do not make sense biologically for these relatively long-lived species---there is no reason to expect a biennial artifact in population abundance.
One consequence of the seesaw effect is that the interannual oscillations can not be reconciled in downstream stock assessments (REFs).

Here, we diagnose the mechanisms underlying seesaw effects, evaluate conditions that modify their strength, and determine what spatiotemporal model configurations can alleviate such effects. 
We do this through simulation experiments and case studies. 
We first simulate data with known underlying properties and apply a suite of spatiotemporal model configurations to assess the properties of estimated indices of abundance compared to the known truth. 
We then apply what we learn from the simulation experiments to a sequence of TODO case studies from Canada, Norway, and the U.S. to demonstrate the process of diagnosing the presence of seesaw effects and evaluating the ability for specific spatiotemporal model configurations to reduce or eliminate seesaw effects.

\section*{Methods}

\subsection*{Simulation experiment}

We conducted a simulation experiment to identify the conditions that induce, strengthen, or weaken seesaw effects, and to evaluate how a range of spatiotemporal model configurations perform across those conditions.
We structured the simulation around a base case, with individual variables then adjusted upward and downward from that baseline (Table~\ref{tab:sim-scenarios}). 
For example, the base case included 200 observations per year; we also tested scenarios in which this number was doubled and halved. 
Parameters in the base case were chosen to reflect typical conditions in groundfish trawl survey datasets often fitted on the west coast of Canada \citep[e.g.,][]{andersonReproducibleData2019}.

Our base case simulation had a strong linear gradient in density in log space from north to south, a 12-year time series of annual effects that followed an AR(1) process, and spatial and spatiotemporal GMRFs representing additional spatially correlated latent effects.
Specifically, it had a spatial GMRF that was constant through time and spatiotemporal GMRFs that were drawn independently each year but with a shared variance among years. 
The spatial correlation range was shared between the spatial and spatiotemporal GMRFs, but the spatial and spatiotemporal components had separate variances.
We simulated a true underlying density on a 100 $\times$ 100 cell grid covering x and y coordinates of 0--1.
From the true grid of density values, we calculated the area-weighted biomass index by multiplying each grid-cell density by the constant cell area and summing across grid cells.
We repeated each simulation scenario for 100 iterations, which was sufficient to achieve stable results.

Given the true density grid, we randomly selected sampling locations from the scenario-specific sampling domain in each survey year and observed the corresponding grid-cell densities with added Tweedie observation error.
We implemented several biennial rotating survey designs, focusing on a north-south sampling scheme for simplicity.
In the biennial scenarios, samples were taken only in the north in odd years and only in the south in even years (Figure TODO). 
We compared designs with and without a gap between the north and south regions, and benchmarked these against a reference scenario in which the full domain was sampled every year. 
In one scenario, we sampled only the last year with full domain coverage to evaluate whether a single year of full coverage could mitigate the seesaw effect.

We fit a sequence of estimation models to the observed values (Table~\ref{tab:model-scenarios}).
These ranged from the standard independent year effects with spatial fields and IID spatiotemporal fields to models with autoregressive temporal and/or spatiotemporal structures (Table~\ref{tab:model-scenarios}).
After fitting a model, we estimated an area-weighted biomass index for each year by predicting on the full survey grid, multiplying each prediction by the constant grid cell area, and summing across grid cells.
We applied a generic bias correction to account for bias and skew due to the non-linear transformation of the random effects \citep{thorsonImplementingGeneric2016}.

We measured several metrics comparing the estimated and true biomass indices for each scenario.
First, we measured a ``seesaw index'' $\psi$, which we defined as:
\begin{equation}
\psi = \left\lvert \overline{r}_{\text{odd}} - \overline{r}_{\text{even}} \right\rvert,
\label{eq:seesaw}
\end{equation}
\noindent
where $r_t = \log(\hat{I}_t / I_t)$ is the log-residual of the estimated biomass index $\hat{I}_t$ in year $t$ against the true index $I_t$.
Subscripts on $\overline{r}$ denote odd and even years, which for the biennial sampling designs considered correspond to the years in which only the north or south region was sampled, respectively.
We calculated $\psi$ for all years, including years with full spatial sampling coverage.
The seesaw index $\psi$ is larger when there is a strong oscillation in the biomass index between years with north vs.\ south coverage.
Taking the absolute value ensures the metric is insensitive to which area is covered in odd vs.\ even years.
We also computed the root mean square error of the log-index values as a measure of accuracy, the mean standard error as a measure of estimated uncertainty, and the coverage of the 50\% confidence intervals (CIs) as a measure of uncertainty calibration.
We evaluated coverage using 50\% CIs because intermediate intervals provide greater resolution for comparing calibration among scenarios than wider intervals, whose empirical coverage can be compressed near one.
